Preface

Every game needs rules. Rules lay out the methods, boundaries, and parameters that keep the game play consistent and reasonably fair. Who creates the rules? The game designer. Stories also have rules. Those rules, as determined by the story maker, keeps the story's world and characters that interact with that world behaving consistently.

One or more players play a game by performing actions that are within the defined game rules. For example, Go and Chess. Stories, on the other hand, take the audience on an emotional ride through a narrative. Unlike games, most stories do not involve direct audience participation.

Role-playing games are like two rivers joining at a confluence; a convergence of stories and games. The two branches, stories and games, mix into each other forming a thing that takes the players on an emotional joyride through a story that they help shape and form. Just like improvisational actors, the players play the unscripted role of one or more character.

How does a story move along in a role-playing game? A player who is familiar with the rules and has a story in mind chooses to be the game's storyteller and referee rolled all into one keeps the story flowing. They are typically called a Game Master.

Some role-playing games lean more towards the gaming aspect and the story aspect just adds flavor. Others lean more towards the story aspect. Cavea is a role-playing game that is striving to find a comfortable place somewhere in the middle.

This book, Cavea: Rules of the Stage, covers only the main rules of the game. The story setting books will supply the settings, background, and setting specific rules that build upon the main rules.

Basic Mechanics

People

The game master runs the game, narrate parts of the story, and plays the roles of the antagonists and the supporting characters; a mash-up of a writer, director, and improvisational actor. The players play the lead character roles of a game's story. They bring the protagonists of the story to imaginary life.

The game master does not play against the player.

Player having control of their own characters is what makes the game fun for the players. Player agency is critical. Even when the character's free will has been compromised or taken away due to an event in the story, the player should still be in control of their character. When the character's free will has been compromised, the player should perform the role of the compromised character (see Offensive Situations for exceptions to playing compromised characters).

Any player that encounters that type of situation should speak up and voice their concern. The player is ultimately responsible for their own well being and should leave the game if the offensive situation continues.

Don't be "one of those" kind of game masters. Work with the player and adjust the scene and/or story. Remember that everyone playing should be having fun.

Again, don't be "one of those" kind of game masters.

Dice

Polyhedral dice are an essential part of the basic mechanics of Cavea's rules. Cavea uses a die notation that is common amongst other role-playing games: dN where d means a single die with N number of sides.

How many of each type of die are needed? Two of each at the very minimum. To keep the die rolling from slowing down game play, four or more of each die type is recommended for a player and six or more of each die type is recommended for a game master. A dice cup is also recommended, especially when the level pools get large and also when playing with kids.

Level Pool

To describe the count of one type of die in a level pool, Cavea uses a notation of C:dN where C is the count of dice with N number of sides. The counts of multiple die types are separated by a |. Character Mechanics

Characters in Cavea serve the same purpose as characters played by actors and actresses in a movie, show, or play; characters give stories a reason to exist, a purpose, and a meaning. The players are the actors and a more akin to improvisational actors. They, along with the game master, form the audience.

Advancement Points

Advancement Points (AP) are the currency used to purchase new skills, to improve a known skill, and improve a character's core physical attributes.

There are two types of Advancement Points: General and Specific.

General Advancement Points

General Advancement Points (GAP) can be used to learn new skills, to increase skill level pools and physical attribute level pools. The game master gives out General Advancement Points as a reward. Typically for good role-playing, accomplishing goals, and anything else that makes the game fun. The game master CANNOT take away GAP from a player as a punishment.

And if they do, then you need to seriously think about playing with a different game master. Kelly

Specific Advancement Points

Specific Advancement Points (SAP) can only be used to increase the level pool of the skill or to increase the level pool of one of the physical attributes related to the skill and are gained through either trial and error or spending time with a mentor or teacher.

Trial and Error Method

SAP for a skill can be gained by using that skill. Certain die results from a skill attempt will indicate that an advancement point was gained specifically for that skill. The following table describes the die results from a skill attempt that indicate when Specific Advancement Points are gained:

Specific Advancement Points Gain Conditions
Skill Attempt Die Result SAPs Gained
One or more 2s on any die rolled. 1
All 2s on all dice rolled. 2
All 1s on all dice rolled. 3
The highest face on each die rolled. 4

When a player gains a SAP, they must tell the game master how many SAP were gained.

Mentor/Teacher Method

SAP can be gained by spending time being trained by a mentor or teacher in a skill. Suggested guideline for a skill with a standard die advancement cost progression: 1 SAP per 6 hours of training. No more than 30 total training hours per week.

Physical Characteristic and Attribute Mechanics

A Character has physical characteristics and attributes. In Cavea, a distinction is made between a characteristic and an attribute. A physical characteristic is a non-measurable, distinguishing quality, aspect, or feature. A physical attribute is a measurable, distinguishing quality, aspect, or feature.

Physical Characteristics

Some physical characteristics add flavor to a character and others may impart bonuses or inflict penalties. Most characteristics are optional. Non-optional characteristics will be noted in any Cavea story universe or story settings guides. The game master may also decide which characteristics are required for the story setting.

Core Attributes

The core physical attributes are:

Mental Strength
represents the character's intelligence, learning capacity, and overall mental capability. Can be abbreviated as mSt.

represents the character's will power and mental focus and stamina. Can be abbreviated as mEn.

represents the character's brawn, hardiness, durability, and muscle strength. Can be abbreviated as bSt.

represents the character's fortitude and stamina. Can be abbreviated as bEn.

Skill Mechanics

Skill Attributes

Name

The name of the skill.

Description

The information and details about the skill.

Level Pool

A skill's level pool is the measurement of expertise a character has in that skill.

Related Physical Attributes

A skill's related physical attributes are the character's core physical attributes that can be affected by the use of the skill. For example, frequent use of the Endurance Running will allow the player to spend SAP to improve the character's body endurance attribute.

The set physical attributes that a skill can be potentially related to:

  • Mental Strength
  • Mental Endurance
  • Mental Agility
  • Body Strength
  • Body Endurance
  • Body Agility
Specialization Requirements

Some skills are categorical and encompass a broad scope of knowledge. Categorical type skills require a specialization of the skill to be chosen. For example, the Ground Vehicle Piloting skill is a skill that requires a specialization by vehicle category be chosen: animal drawn cart, personal vehicles, construction equipment, goblin spring hopper, and etc...

Other skills are general, giving a character a general level of confidence in the skill's field of knowledge and specialization is optional. For example, the Music skill will let a character read and critically listen to music. To sing at a professional level, a specialization in singing would need to be chosen. To play a musical instrument, a specialization is a musical instrument category would need to be chosen. Having a level pool in a general skill is not a requirement for choosing a specialization.

Prerequisites

Attributes and skills can be prerequisites for other skills. A skill can one or more prerequisites. If a skill has prerequisites, those prerequisites must be satisfied before the skill can be purchased with advancement points. See Combined To Enhance in the Skill Attempt section for prerequisite skill usage limitations.

Alternative Skills and Attributes

A skill may have an alternative skill and/or attribute. The alternative's level pool with any stated results modifier can be used as the level pool when performing a skill attempt. Any SAP gained in this manner goes to the skill and not the alternative. This is the trial and error method of learning.

Categorizations
Genre Aspects

Skill Attempt

When a person playing a character wants to use a particular skill, in the simplest form, the player rolls the skill's level pool, adds up all of the 1s, adds and/or subtracts the level pool's result modifier, and adds and/or subtracts any relevant situational modifiers forming a skill attempt result. The skill attempt result is then compared with some other value. If the skill attempt result is greater than the other value, then the skill attempt was successful.

Opposing/Opposed Skill Attempt

The intended actions of two or more characters may come into direct conflict with each other. An obvious example is when one character attacks another character. The attacking character wants to inflict damage on the opponent. The opponent wants to defend against receiving any damage. Another example

If the skill attempt is opposing/is opposed by a skill attempt by a separate entity then the total result is compared with the opposing/opposed total result. The highest result wins. The difference between the highest and lowest total results may dictate the type and/or scale of the results. E.g. Defending against a sword attack with a small shield.

Unopposed Skill Attempt

If the skill attempt is unopposed then the GM compares the total result with a predetermined target number. If the total result is higher than or equal to the target number then the skill attempt is successful. If the total result is lower than the target number then the skill attempt failed. The difference between the total result and the target number may dictate the type and/or scale of the success or failure.

Combining Skills

In the real world, multiple skills are needed to successfully perform any significantly complex task. Cavea has three flavors of skill combinations: combined to enhance, combined with, and teamwork.

Combined to Enhance

Sometimes, during a skill attempt, a skill may be enhanced by one or more additional skills. A skill that can be enhanced is called an enhance-able skill. A skill that can enhance another skill is called an enhancive skill. When enhancing a skill with enhancive skills is applicable, the ultimate decision whether or not to apply the enhancive skills during a skill attempt is always at the discretion of the person playing the character.

An enhance-able skill may also be an enhancive skill. If a skill is both enhance-able and enhancive, when it is used as an enhancive skill it, in and of itself, may not be further enhanced.

An enhance-able skill should specify, in its description, the applicable enhancive skills.

An enhancive skill should specify the enhance-able skills that it applies to and the conditions when the enhancement applies.

Unless explicitly stated by the rules or by the game master, prerequisite skills may not be combined to enhance the skill during skill attempts.

To combine one or more enhancive skills to enhance a skill during a skill attempt:

  1. Roll the level pools of the skill and the enhancive skills.
  2. Add up all of the 1s forming a single subtotal.
  3. For each enhancive skill used, subtract 1 from the subtotal.
  4. Use the resulting total as the skill attempt's result.

Combined With

A game master may decide that two or more skills are needed to accomplish a single complex task. To combine multiple skills into a single skill attempt, the level pools for the each of the specified skills are rolled and all of the 1s are added together forming a single result.

For example, using a whitewater kayak to run an unfamiliar large class V rapid, which is difficult and extremely dangerous, requires both the Small Boat Control: Whitewater Kayak specialized skill and the Nautical Navigation: Streams and Rivers specialized skill.

Teamwork

Similar to combining skills, the GM may decide that teamwork is needed to accomplish a single task. To attempt a skill as a team, each gamer rolls the level pool for their character's skill and all of the 1s are added together forming a single result.

Skill Aquirement and Improvement

As mentioned in the Advancement Points section, advancement points are used to purchase new skills and to improve existing skills.

A character's fundamental set of skills is acquired through the early adolescent phase of the character creation process. Fundamental skills are improved and new skills are acquired through the later adolescent phase of the character creation process. Depending on the setting, groups of skills can be acquired during the apprenticeship and advanced education phase of the character creation process. The game master will specify the amount of GAP to use for character creation.

Existing skills can be improved and new skills can be acquired as the character is played. Both GAP and SAP are used.

Health and Damage Mechanics

In some stories, a character's life is rough. This can be especially true for a player's character. As in real life, a character can be healthy, invigorated, fatigued, sick, roughed up, disabled, or on the brink of death. A character's well being is tracked in the following ways: Health Points, Injuries, and Disabilities.

Health Points

Health Points (HP) is a measure of a character's health, well being, and level of exhaustion. When a character's Health Points reaches 0 that character dies; croaked—kaput—goner—bye bye—"I get his boots!"—kind of dead.

Calculating Health Points

The following describes how to calculate a character's health points.

  1. Find the Level Pool Die to Health Points table that is appropriate for the character's species, race, culture, and age.
  2. For each die in the character's mSt, mEn, mAg, bSt, bEn, and bAg attribute level pools:
    1. Look up the Health Point value for the type of die in the table, and
    2. add the value to the character's Health Point total.

Disabilities

A disability that impairs a character are:

  • temporary or permanent,
  • can be classified mental or physical, and
  • rated as moderate, major, or severe.

A temporary disability is a disability that will eventually go away. The duration of recovery time is measured in years.

A disability can also be rated as minor, but it does not impair the character. A minor disability, as it pertains to the game, is considered to be character flavoring.

Disabilities and Health Points are separate. A character can be disabled and healthy.

Damage

Damage happens. A hero may get shot at, beaten up, fall out of a tree, stabbed with a sword, hit with a car, dropped out of an airplane, or vented out an airlock. An adventurer falls into a spiked pit, gets chewed on by piraas, eats an under cooked chicken leg from a road side vendor, or gets blasted by the acid spray of a gorganuan marsh dragon.

Damage Type

Penetration
Damage caused by piercing and penetrating into the body.
Incision
Damage caused by cutting or slicing into the body.
Laceration
Damage caused by tearing or ripping into the body.
Blunt force
Damage caused by an impact into the body.
Burn
Damage caused by heat (thermal), chemicals, electricity, or radiation.
Amputation/Avulsion
Damage caused by the separation of a limb or appendage from the body.

Damage Dice

Damage dice is the measure of the potential amount of damage than can be dealt. The amount of damage dealt differs depending on the game's genre and style. To represent the different damage styles, either the setting/universe book or the game master will specify the die type used for damage. The type of damage die can be one of: d4, d6, d8, or d10. Once chosen for a game or campaign, the type of damage die should not be changed unless the game master and all players agree to make the change.

Equipment Mechanics

Armor

Armor absorbs damage points. Similar to health points, armor has a set number of armor points that are depleted as damage is absorbed.
