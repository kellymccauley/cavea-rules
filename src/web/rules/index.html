Preface

Every game needs rules. Rules lay out the methods, boundaries, and parameters that keep the game play consistent and reasonably fair. Who creates the rules? The game designer. Stories also have rules. Those rules, as determined by the story maker, keeps the story's world and characters that interact with that world behaving consistently.

One or more players play a game by performing actions that are within the defined game rules. For example, Go and Chess. Stories, on the other hand, take the audience on an emotional ride through a narrative. Unlike games, most stories do not involve direct audience participation.

Role-playing games are like two rivers joining at a confluence; a convergence of stories and games. The two branches, stories and games, mix into each other forming a thing that takes the players on an emotional joyride through a story that they help shape and form. Just like improvisational actors, the players play the unscripted role of one or more character.

How does a story move along in a role-playing game? A player who is familiar with the rules and has a story in mind chooses to be the game's storyteller and referee rolled all into one keeps the story flowing. They are typically called a Game Master.

Some role-playing games lean more towards the gaming aspect and the story aspect just adds flavor. Other role-playing games lean more towards the story aspect.

Cavea is a role-playing game that strives to find a comfortable place somewhere in the middle. This book, Cavea: Rules of the Stage, covers only the rules of the game. Other books will mix in the story flavors.

Basic Mechanics

Dice

Polyhedral dice are an essential part of the basic mechanics of Cavea's rules. Cavea uses a die notation that is common amongst other role-playing games: dN where d means a single die with N number of sides.

The following types of die are required during game play: d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, and d20.

How many of each type of die are needed? Two of each at the very minimum. To keep the die rolling from slowing down game play, four or more of each die type is recommended for a player and six or more of each die type is recommended for a game master.

Level Pool

A Level Pool (LP) is:

  • a measurement of expertise of a skill or
  • a measurement of physical ability.

A Level Pool consists of one or more dice and sometimes a result modifier. Each die in the pool is either a d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, or d20.

To describe the count of one type of die in a level pool, Cavea uses a notation of C:dN where C is the count of dice with N number of sides. The counts of multiple die types are separated by a |. Rolling a Level Pool

The procedure for rolling a Level Pool is as follow:

  1. Gather and roll all of the dice that compose the Level Pool.
  2. Add up all of the 1s for an unmodified total.
  3. Add and/or subtract any relevant modifiers to/from the unmodified total for the final result.

Advancement Points

Advancement Points (AP) are the currency used to purchase new skills, to increase a skill's Level Pool, and improve physical attributes.

There are two types of Advancement Points: General and Specific.

General Advancement Points

The General Advancement Points (GAP) can be used to learn new skills and to increase the Level Pools of known skills.

Specific Advancement Points

The Specific Advancement Points (SAP) can only be used to either increase the Level Pool of a specific skill or to increase the Level Pool of one of the physical attributes related to that skill. Specific Advancement Points are gained when the dice in the Level Pool are rolled during a skill attempt. A single SAP is gained for that skill if one or more 2s are rolled.

Skill Attempt

A gamer rolls the skill's Level Pool when attempting to use the skill. The gamer then adds up all of the 1s, and adds and/or subtracts any relevant modifiers. If every rolled die in the Level Pool results in a 1, then 2 Specific Advancement Points are gained for that skill.

Opposing/Opposed Skill Attempt

If the skill attempt is opposing/is opposed by a skill attempt by a separate entity then the total result is compared with the opposing/opposed total result. The highest result wins. The difference between the highest and lowest total results may dictate the type and/or scale of the results. E.g. Defending against a sword attack with a small shield.

Unopposed Skill Attempt

If the skill attempt is unopposed then the GM compares the total result with a predetermined target number. If the total result is higher than or equal to the target number then the skill attempt is successful. If the total result is lower than the target number then the skill attempt failed. The difference between the total result and the target number may dictate the type and/or scale of the success or failure.

Combining Skills

The GM may decide that more the one skill is needed to accomplish a single task. To combine multiple skills, the Level Pools for the each skill are rolled and all of the 1s are added together forming a single result.

Teamwork

Similar to combining skills, the GM may decide that teamwork is needed to accomplish a single task. To attempt a skill as a team, each gamer rolls the Level Pool for their character's skill and all of the 1s are added together forming a single result.

Skill Acquirement

Skills are acquired during the character creation process and while playing the character.

Acquirement During Character Creation

A character's fundamental set of skills are acquired through the early adolescent phase of the character creation process. Fundamental skills are improved and new skills are acquired through the later adolescent phase of the character creation process. Depending on the setting, groups of skills can be acquired during the apprenticeship and advanced education phase of the character creation process.

Acquirement During Character Play

Skills are acquired as the character is played. General Advancement Points are used to purchase skills.

Physical Attribute Mechanics

The core physical attributes are:

Mental Strength
represents the character's intelligence, learning capacity, and overall mental capability.
Abbreviated as mSt.
Mental Endurance
represents the character's will power and mental focus and stamina.
Abbreviated as mEn.
Mental Agility
represents the character's speed of memory recall, the speed at which learning occurs, and the level of complexity of new topics that can be learned.
Abbreviated as mAg.
Body Strength
represents the character's brawn, hardiness, durability, and muscle strength.
Abbreviated as bSt.
Body Endurance
represents the character's fortitude and stamina.
Abbreviated as bEn.
Body Agility
represents the character's balance, dexterity, and nimbleness.
Abbreviated as bAg.

Similar to skills, Level Pools are used as a measure of a core physical attribute.

Physical Attribute Level Pool Advancement

All physical attributes start out with a 1:d20 LP.

Health and Damage Mechanics

Calculating Health Points

For each die in the mSt, mEn, mAg, bSt, bEn, and bAg Level Pools, look up the Health Point value for the type of die in the following table and add the value to the character's Health Point total.

Level Pool Die to Health Points Table
Die Health Points
d20 1
d12 3
d10 4
d8 6
d6 9
d4 12
Example

mSt: 1:d8|2:d12|1:d20
mEn: 1:d12|4:d20
mAg: 2:d10|2:d12|1:d20
bSt: 2:d6|1:d10|2:d12|1:d20
bEn: 3:d6|2:d12|2:d20
bAg: 1:d4|2:d6|4:d20

mSt: 6 + (2 * 3) + 1 = 13
mEn: 3 + 4 = 7
mAg: (2 * 4) + (2 * 3) + 1 = 15
bSt: (2 * 6) + 4 + (2 * 3) + 1 = 23
bEn: (3 * 9) + (2 * 3) + 2 = 35
bAg: 12 + (2 * 9) + 4 = 34

13 + 7 + 15 + 23 + 35 + 34 = 127 Health Points
