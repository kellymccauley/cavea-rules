Preface

Every game needs rules. Rules lay out the methods, boundaries, and parameters that keep the game play consistent and reasonably fair. Who creates the rules? The game designer. Stories also have rules. Those rules, as determined by the story maker, keeps the story's world and characters that interact with that world behaving consistently.

One or more players play a game by performing actions that are within the defined game rules. For example, Go and Chess. Stories, on the other hand, take the audience on an emotional ride through a narrative. Unlike games, most stories do not involve direct audience participation.

Role-playing games are like two rivers joining at a confluence; a convergence of stories and games. The two branches, stories and games, mix into each other forming a thing that takes the players on an emotional joyride through a story that they help shape and form. Just like improvisational actors, the players play the unscripted role of one or more character.

How does a story move along in a role-playing game? A player who is familiar with the rules and has a story in mind chooses to be the game's storyteller and referee rolled all into one keeps the story flowing. They are typically called a Game Master.

Some role-playing games lean more towards the gaming aspect and the story aspect just adds flavor. Other role-playing games lean more towards the story aspect.

Cavea is a role-playing game that strives to find a comfortable place somewhere in the middle. This book, Cavea: Rules of the Stage, covers only the rules of the game. Other books will mix in the story flavors.

Basic Mechanics

People

At least two people are needed to play Cavea.

One person needs to be the game master. The game master runs the game, narrate parts of the story, and plays the roles of the antagonists and the supporting characters; a mashup of a writer, director, and improvisational actor.

At least one person needs to be the player.

The game master does not play against the player. Cavea is not and should not be played as a Game Master vs. Player game.

Dice

Polyhedral dice are an essential part of the basic mechanics of Cavea's rules. Cavea uses a die notation that is common amongst other role-playing games: dN where d means a single die with N number of sides.

The following types of die are required during game play: d4, d6, d8, and d10.

How many of each type of die are needed? Two of each at the very minimum. To keep the die rolling from slowing down game play, four or more of each die type is recommended for a player and six or more of each die type is recommended for a game master. A dice cup is also recommended, especially when the level pools get large and also when playing with kids.

Character Mechanics

Characters in Cavea serve the same purpose as characters played by actors and actresses, they give stories a meaning. The players are the actors; technically they are improvisational actors. They, along with the game master, are also the audience.

Players play the roles of the lead characters of a game's story. They are the stars of the story. They bring the protagonists to imaginary life.

A player's character's actions, reactions, decisions, and spoken lines are not and should never be scripted or predetermined by the game master or another player. This is even true when the character's free will has been compromised or taken away in the story. A good player will perfom the role of the compromised character.

Level Pool

A level pool (LP) is:

  • a measurement of expertise of a skill or
  • a measurement of physical ability.

A level pool consists of one or more dice and sometimes a result modifier. Each die in the pool is either a d4, d6, d8, or d10.

To describe the count of one type of die in a level pool, Cavea uses a notation of C:dN where C is the count of dice with N number of sides. The counts of multiple die types are separated by a |. Rolling a Level Pool

The procedure for rolling a level pool is as follow:

  1. Gather and roll all of the dice that compose the level pool.
  2. Add up all of the 1s for an unmodified total.
  3. Add and/or subtract any relevant modifiers to/from the unmodified total for the final result.

Advancement Points

Advancement Points (AP) are the currency used to purchase new skills, to increase a skill's level pool, and improve physical attributes.

There are two types of Advancement Points: General and Specific.

General Advancement Points

The General Advancement Points (GAP) can be used to learn new skills and to increase the level pools of known skills. The game master gives out General Advancement Points as a reward. Typically for good role-playing, accomplishing goals, and anything else that makes the game fun. The game master CANNOT take away GAP from a player as a punishment.

And if they do, then you need to seriously think about playing with a different game master. Kelly

Specific Advancement Points

The Specific Advancement Points (SAP) are advancement points that are gained by skill use. Certain die results from a skill attempt will indicate that an advancement point was gained specifically for that skill. That gained SAP then can only be used to increase the level pool of the skill or to increase the level pool of one of the physical attributes related to the skill.

The following table describes the die results from a skill attempt that indicate when Specific Advancement Points are gained:

Skill Attempt Results SAPs Gained
One or more 2s on any die rolled. 1
All 2s on all dice rolled. 2
All 1s on all dice rolled. 3
The highest face on each die rolled. 4

When a player gains a SAP, they must tell the game master how many SAP were gained.

Skill Attempt

A gamer rolls the skill's level pool when attempting to use the skill. The gamer then adds up all of the 1s, and adds and/or subtracts any relevant modifiers.

Opposing/Opposed Skill Attempt

If the skill attempt is opposing/is opposed by a skill attempt by a separate entity then the total result is compared with the opposing/opposed total result. The highest result wins. The difference between the highest and lowest total results may dictate the type and/or scale of the results. E.g. Defending against a sword attack with a small shield.

Unopposed Skill Attempt

If the skill attempt is unopposed then the GM compares the total result with a predetermined target number. If the total result is higher than or equal to the target number then the skill attempt is successful. If the total result is lower than the target number then the skill attempt failed. The difference between the total result and the target number may dictate the type and/or scale of the success or failure.

Combining Skills

The GM may decide that more the one skill is needed to accomplish a single task. To combine multiple skills, the level pools for the each skill are rolled and all of the 1s are added together forming a single result.

Teamwork

Similar to combining skills, the GM may decide that teamwork is needed to accomplish a single task. To attempt a skill as a team, each gamer rolls the level pool for their character's skill and all of the 1s are added together forming a single result.

Skill Acquirement

Skills are acquired during the character creation process and while playing the character.

Acquirement During Character Creation

A character's fundamental set of skills are acquired through the early adolescent phase of the character creation process. Fundamental skills are improved and new skills are acquired through the later adolescent phase of the character creation process. Depending on the setting, groups of skills can be acquired during the apprenticeship and advanced education phase of the character creation process.

Acquirement During Character Play

Skills are acquired as the character is played. General Advancement Points are used to purchase skills.

Health and Damage Mechanics

In some stories, a character's life is rough. This can be especially true for a player's character. As in real life, a character can be healthy, invigorated, fatigued, sick, roughed up, disabled, or on the brink of death. A character's well being is tracked in the following ways: Health Points, Injuries, and Disabilities.

Health Points

Health Points (HP) is a measure of a character's health, well being, and level of exhaustion. When a character's Health Points reaches 0 that character dies; croaked—kaput—goner—bye bye—"I get his boots!"—kind of dead.

Calculating Health Points

The following describes how to calculate a character's health points.

  1. Find the Level Pool Die to Health Points table that is appropriate for the character's species, race, culture, and age.
  2. For each die in the character's mSt, mEn, mAg, bSt, bEn, and bAg attribute level pools:
    1. Look up the Health Point value for the type of die in the table, and
    2. add the value to the character's Health Point total.

Injuries

Disabilites

A disability that impairs a character are:

  • temporary or permanent,
  • can be classified mental or physical, and
  • rated as moderate, major, or severe.

A temporary disability is a disability that will eventually go away. The duration of recovery time is measured in years.

A disability can also be rated as minor, but it does not impair the character. A minor disability, as it pertains to the game, is considered to be character flavoring.

Disabilities and Health Points are separate. A character can be disabled and healthy.

Damage

Damage happens. A hero may get shot at, beaten up, fall out of a tree, stabbed with a sword, hit with a car, dropped out of an airplane, or vented out an airlock. An adventurer falls into a spiked pit, gets chewed on by pirañas, eats an under cooked chicken leg from a road side vendor, or gets blasted by the acid spray of a gorganuan marsh dragon.
